## 1. Core Boundary: This Is Not CO2 Denial

This framework does **not** deny that anthropogenic CO2 emissions are a major driver of global warming.

Human activities, principally through greenhouse gas emissions, have caused global warming. CO2 remains a central long-lived greenhouse gas and must be reduced.

However, this framework rejects an oversimplified **CO2-only framing** in which climate stabilization is treated as if it were only a matter of reducing emissions.

The climate system also depends on:

- carbon fixation and absorption systems
- forests, soils, wetlands, and microbial cycles
- ocean circulation and ocean CO2 uptake
- plankton systems and marine biological productivity
- water circulation, clouds, rain, evaporation, and transpiration
- land-use change, ecosystem degradation, and soil carbon loss
- heat accumulation and delayed thermal response in the ocean

Therefore, the correct boundary is:

> CO2 emissions reduction is necessary, but not sufficient by itself. Climate stabilization also requires restoring carbon sinks, biological circulation, water cycles, soil systems, ocean circulation, and ecosystem-based cooling functions.

## 2. Why the CO2-only Frame Is Incomplete

A CO2-only frame tends to focus on the atmospheric input side:

- fossil fuel emissions
- atmospheric CO2 increase
- radiative forcing
- warming

This is real, but incomplete.

A fuller climate-stability frame must also include the sink and circulation side:

- forests, soils, oceans, plankton, wetlands, and microbial cycles
- carbon fixation and absorption
- water circulation and evaporative cooling
- ecosystem stability
- climate regulation

When fixation and absorption systems degrade, the same emissions can produce greater atmospheric accumulation and greater instability.

When water cycles, vegetation, ocean mixing, and soil moisture decline, the Earth loses part of its natural cooling capacity.

This framework therefore treats climate change not only as an emissions problem, but also as a **circulation-collapse problem**.

## 5. Key Concept Map

Human emissions increase atmospheric greenhouse gases, which increases radiative forcing and warming.

Carbon sink degradation reduces land and ocean uptake, which can increase atmospheric accumulation.

Ocean warming and stratification can weaken mixing, alter nutrient circulation, and reduce biological productivity in some regions.

Soil and vegetation degradation can reduce carbon storage, water retention, evapotranspiration cooling, and fire resistance.

Water-cycle disruption can weaken cooling through evaporation, clouds, rain, and vegetation.

Direct Planetary Cooling proposes to restore or support natural cooling and circulation functions while emissions reduction and sink restoration continue.

## 6. Scientific Anchors

The following points are compatible with mainstream climate science:

1. Human activities, principally through greenhouse gas emissions, have caused global warming.
2. CO2 is a central long-lived greenhouse gas and remains a major driver of warming.
3. Natural sinks such as land ecosystems and oceans remove a large fraction of human-emitted CO2, but not all of it.
4. Land and ocean carbon sinks are affected by climate variability, warming, circulation, drought, fire, and ecosystem change.
5. Climate response must include mitigation, adaptation, ecosystem restoration, and risk governance.
